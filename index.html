Summary of Key Laws

RA 9710

Magna Carta of Women
Protects women’s rights, ensures equality in government policies, and guarantees access to education, health, and livelihood.

Read official law text

RA 6725

Anti-Discrimination in Employment
Bans sexual orientation and gender identity discrimination in hiring, pay, promotion, and workplace benefits.

Read official law text

RA 7192

Women in Development Act
Promotes equal participation of women in economic development and government programs.

Read official law text

RA 7877

Anti-Sexual Harassment Act
Defines and prevents sexual harassment in workplaces, schools, and training centers.

Read official law text

RA 7600

Breastfeeding Act
Requires lactation stations and protects a mother’s right to breastfeed in public and at work.

Read official law text

RA 7322

Maternity Benefits
Ensures paid maternity leave for female workers and employer compliance.

Read official law text

RA 9262

Anti-VAWC
Protects victims of violence against women and their children through legal remedies and support services.

Read official law text

RA 8353

Anti-Rape Law
Expands the definition of rape, increases penalties, and strengthens prosecution of sexual violence.

Read official law text

RA 7822

Anti-Trafficking
Combats human trafficking, especially of women and children, and provides protection for victims.

Read official law text

RA 7688

Women in Military
Allows women to enlist and serve in the Philippine military, promoting gender inclusion in defense.

Read official law text

RA 10354

Reproductive Health Law
Guarantees access to family planning, maternal care, and reproductive health education.

SOGIE Bill

Proposed Anti-Discrimination Law
Aims to protect all individuals from discrimination based on sexual orientation, gender identity, and expression.

Who These Laws Protect

Women and girls in workplace, health, education, and family settings.

Pregnant and breastfeeding mothers.

Victims of sexual violence, harassment, abuse, and trafficking.

Workers facing discrimination for gender, sexuality, or marital status.

LGBTQIA+ individuals under proposed SOGIE protections.

Real-Life Application

Scenario 1: Workplace Harassment

Maria reports unwanted comments from a supervisor. Under RA 7877, her employer must investigate, take corrective action, and protect her from retaliation.

RA 6725 also supports her if discrimination occurs because of gender identity or sexual orientation.

Scenario 2: Domestic Violence

Anna seeks help after physical abuse by her partner. RA 9262 allows her to get a barangay protection order and access legal and support services.

The law covers her children as protected persons and helps her safely rebuild her life.

Scenario 3: Pregnancy and Breastfeeding

Jessa returns to work after childbirth. RA 7322 gives her paid maternity leave, and RA 7600 requires her employer to provide a lactation station.

This helps her balance work and maternal health without losing income or dignity.

Scenario 4: Access to Reproductive Health

The rural health clinic offers family planning counseling and prenatal care under RA 10354, helping women make informed reproductive choices.

The law supports access to safe maternal services even in underserved communities.

Scenario 5: Anti-Trafficking Protection

Rhea escapes a trafficking recruiter and seeks help. RA 7822 provides victim support, rehabilitation, and prosecution of traffickers.

This law helps prevent exploitation and gives survivors access to shelter and legal aid.

Scenario 6: Women in Military Service

Grace applies to join the armed forces. RA 7688 supports women’s entry into military service and promotes equal opportunity in uniformed roles.

The law reinforces that women can serve in defense and leadership positions.

Critical Reflection

How these laws promote gender equality

  • They make discrimination illegal and create legal remedies for victims.
  • They support women’s participation in the workforce, leadership, and security.
  • They protect reproductive choices and workplace rights, which are central to equality.
  • They recognize that gender-based violence is a barrier to equal opportunity.

Remaining issues despite these laws

  • Enforcement gaps: many women and LGBTQIA+ people still cannot access justice promptly.
  • Cultural stigma and lack of awareness prevent victims from reporting abuse.
  • Legal protection is incomplete: the SOGIE bill is still proposed and not yet enacted.
  • Economic inequality, unequal healthcare access, and violence persist.
  • Intersectional issues such as poverty, disability, and indigenous rights need stronger support.

Closing

These laws form a foundation for gender justice in the Philippines, but real progress depends on stronger implementation, public education, and inclusive protection for all identities.
